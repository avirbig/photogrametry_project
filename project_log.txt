ENTRY 0  -  PROJECT GOAL AND THE BIG IDEA          [date: 2026-07-06]
================================================================================

WHAT WE ARE TRYING TO DO
------------------------
We have 14 old photographs of the same building, taken from different
positions. By hand we marked "matching points" - the same physical feature
(a window corner, a roof edge) clicked in every photo where it is visible.

From those 2D pixel marks ALONE, we want to recover four things:
  1. Where each marked point sits in 3D space  -> its (X, Y, Z) coordinates.
  2. Where each camera was  -> the 3D position each photo was taken from.
  3. Which way each camera pointed  -> its 3D orientation.
  4. How much to trust each result  -> an error value for every point/camera.

At the end, everything is expressed relative to the marked points themselves:
the points sit at the centre of the coordinate system and the cameras are
placed around them. This whole family of techniques is called
"photogrammetry" or "Structure from Motion" (SfM) = working out 3D structure
from a set of ordinary photos.

THE ONE IDEA BEHIND EVERYTHING
------------------------------
A camera takes a 3D point and flattens it onto a flat picture. If you knew
exactly where the camera was, which way it pointed, and how zoomed-in it was,
you could PREDICT the exact pixel where any 3D point would land.

The reverse of that prediction is our entire task: starting from the pixels we
clicked, work backwards to recover the 3D points and every camera's position,
orientation, and zoom.

The compact way this is written (we will unpack every symbol later) is:

      x  ~  K [ R | t ] X

  X = the 3D point            (what we want)
  x = the pixel it lands on    (what we measured by clicking)
  R = camera orientation       (which way it points)  (what we want)
  t = tied to camera position  (where it sits)         (what we want)
  K = the camera's internal settings (mainly the zoom / focal length)

Don't worry about this line yet. Each piece gets its own entry with real
numbers when we reach the step that uses it.

ENTRY 1  -  THE 9-STEP PLAN AND WHERE WE STAND     [date: 2026-07-06]
================================================================================

The project follows the 9 steps in the guide "From Old Photos to 3D".

  STEP                                              STATUS
  ------------------------------------------------  -----------------
  1. Mark matching points by hand                   DONE
  2. Build the who-saw-what table of pixels         DONE
  3. Set camera intrinsics (centre + square pixels, DONE (assumption)
     let the zoom/focal length be estimated)        pending in code
  4. Seed pair: pick best 2 photos, solve their     NOT STARTED
     relative position and orientation
  5. Triangulate the first cloud of 3D points       NOT STARTED
  6. Add remaining photos one at a time             NOT STARTED
     (resection + triangulation)
  7. Bundle adjustment: refine all cameras and      NOT STARTED
     points together (least-squares best fit)
  8. Read off the error values                      NOT STARTED
  9. Re-express everything in the points' own       NOT STARTED
     coordinate frame (centre + spread directions)

We have completed Steps 1-2. The rest of this logbook records Steps 3-9 as we
build them, by hand, in plain linear algebra.

Before building the solver we checked whether the marked data is good enough
to actually reconstruct 3D. Summary: the data is STRONG and clearly solvable.

HEADLINE NUMBERS
----------------
  Distinct 3D points marked (P) . . . . . . 34   (labelled P0 ... P33)
  Photos (N) . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . 14
  Total clicks / measurements (M) . . . . . 279  (one click = one point in one photo)

IS THE PUZZLE SOLVABLE? (a counting check)
------------------------------------------
The guide gives a rule: the number of things we MEASURED must be at least the
number of things we DON'T KNOW.

  Each click gives 2 numbers (its x and y), so measurements = 2 * M = 2 * 279 = 558.

  Unknowns we must solve for:
     7 per camera  (6 for position+orientation, 1 for unknown zoom) -> 7 * 14
     3 per 3D point (its X, Y, Z)                                    -> 3 * 34
     minus 7        (the scene can be freely slid/turned/resized)
     = 7*14 + 3*34 - 7 = 98 + 102 - 7 = 193

  Check:  measurements 558  >=  unknowns 193.   PASS, with large margin.

A margin this big is exactly what we want, because scanned historical photos
are noisy and we need spare measurements to average that noise out.

VIEWS PER POINT (how many photos each point appears in)
-------------------------------------------------------
Rule from the guide: every point should appear in at least 3 photos (2 is the
bare minimum to get depth; the 3rd lets us cross-check for mistakes).

  28 of 34 points appear in 3 or more photos.  GOOD.

ENTRY 4  -  DECISIONS MADE AND TOOLS CHOSEN        [date: 2026-07-06]

DECISION 1 - Method: hand-rolled linear algebra (not a black-box library)
    Because this is a learning project for the course, we implement the maths
    ourselves (the 8-point algorithm, triangulation, resection, bundle
    adjustment) rather than calling a ready-made photogrammetry library. At
    each step this logbook will show the formula, the actual matrices with our
    real numbers, and a plain-words explanation of every operation.

DECISION 2 - Logbook format: plain text (.txt)
    Chosen for easy reading/editing in Notepad++ on Windows, with clear
    ASCII section separators. Can be converted to a formatted report later.

DECISION 3 - One logbook, with a backlog at the bottom
    This single file is the chronological record (the "logbook"). A short
    "NEXT STEPS" list at the very end acts as the to-do list (the "backlog").

STILL TO DECIDE (open questions)
    - Whether we have any known real-world distance between two points, which
      would let us fix the true SCALE of the model (Step 9). Without it the
      shape is correct but the overall size is arbitrary. -> user to check.
    - Which linear-algebra helper library to allow. Even "by hand" we will
      need basic matrix operations and SVD (a standard matrix tool, explained
      when first used). numpy is already in requirements.txt and is the natural
      choice for the arithmetic, while we still write all the photogrammetry
      logic ourselves. -> proposed: numpy only, no vision library.

ENVIRONMENT NOTE
    The system Python (3.14) currently has no packages installed, so a virtual
    environment with numpy (and matplotlib/Pillow for the existing tools) will
    be set up before running any code.

ENTRY 5  -  DATA CLEANUP AND SCALE DECISION        [date: 2026-07-06]
================================================================================

TWO open items from Entry 4 are now resolved.

ACTION 1 - Deleted point P26
    P26 was marked in only ONE photo (09_diagonal_left_bw.jpg, at pixel
    (630, 122)). A point needs at least TWO photos to recover its depth, so a
    one-photo point carries no usable 3D information. Decision: delete it.
    - Removed P26 from data/correspondences.json and data/correspondences.csv.
    - The remaining 33 point IDs keep their original labels (P0..P25, P27..P33).
      We deliberately did NOT renumber them, so every pixel already recorded
      still refers to the exact same physical feature. There is simply no "P26"
      anymore - a harmless gap in the numbering.

    Updated totals after deletion:
        Distinct 3D points (P) . . . 33   (was 34)
        Total clicks (M) . . . . . . 278  (was 279)
        Solvability re-check: 2M = 556  >=  unknowns 7*14 + 3*33 - 7 = 190. PASS.
    All other findings in Entry 3 are unchanged.

ACTION 2 - No real-world distances available (scale is left arbitrary)
    The original building's true measurements are not available to us. In
    Step 9 the "scale" of the model is normally fixed by ONE known real
    distance between two points (e.g. a measured window height). Without it:
        - The SHAPE of the reconstruction is still fully correct (all angles
          and all length RATIOS are right).
        - Only the overall SIZE is undetermined - the model could be scaled up
          or down uniformly and still match every photo equally well.
    Consequence for the report: 3D coordinates and camera positions will be
    given in arbitrary units (not metres). If a real distance is found later,
    the whole model can be rescaled to metres in one multiplication.
